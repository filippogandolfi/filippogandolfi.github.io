If you had told me a couple of years ago that my path would cross the Olympic Games, I would have guessed it involved a robot, a test bench, or — in my wildest dreams — a pair of goalkeeper gloves. Instead, it involved a stopwatch and a sheet of ice.

## How it started: Turin 2025

In 2025 I joined the curling world as a **Timer** at the **University Winter Games in Turin**. I walked in knowing curling the way most Italians do — as that hypnotic sport that appears on TV every four years — and walked out genuinely in love with it.

What surprised me most is that curling is a game *made of time*. Teams don't just play ends; they spend a budget of thinking time, and someone has to run those clocks with total precision while the stones are sliding and the skips are shouting. That someone became me. It's a role that rewards exactly the things I do at work every day: focus, calm under pressure, and an unhealthy affection for measuring things precisely.

## Then the call: the Olympics

And then came 2026. **Official timekeeper at the Milano-Cortina Olympic Games.**

Writing that sentence still feels strange. The Games were at home, in Italy, and I got to be part of the machine that makes them run — one tiny, punctual gear in an enormous clockwork. The level is different in every way: the athletes, the crowd, the broadcast pressure, the awareness that a clock error isn't a quiet mistake but one with the whole world watching.

The job itself is beautifully humble. Nobody notices the timekeeper when everything goes right — and that's precisely the point. It's the same philosophy I bring to testing software: the best quality work is invisible, and you only see it when it's missing.

## What I'm taking home

- Precision is a craft, whether the thing being measured is a screen transition at 240 fps or a skip's last seconds of thinking time.
- Volunteering in sport gives back more than it takes. Twice now it has put me inside events I used to watch from a couch.
- Curling people are some of the warmest people in sport. *Good curling* isn't just a phrase — it's a whole attitude.

From a science festival in Genoa, to a driving simulator for a hospital, to the Olympic ice: apparently my career strategy is saying yes to interesting rooms and figuring out the rest once I'm inside.

A presto — and good curling. 🥌
